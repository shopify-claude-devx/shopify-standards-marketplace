# Article Writer

Writes high-quality articles that read like they were written by a skilled human writer — not generated by AI. Produces Markdown files with structured frontmatter, following a disciplined writing methodology.

## Workflow

Follow these five phases in order for every article:

### Phase 1: Clarify

Before writing anything, ask the user these questions using `AskUserQuestion`:

1. **Topic & angle** — What specific topic should the article cover? What's the unique angle or perspective?
2. **Audience** — Who is reading this? (developers, general public, business professionals, students, etc.)
3. **Tone preference** — Casual and friendly, professional but warm, or authoritative and polished?
4. **Key points** — Are there specific points, arguments, or sections that must be included?
5. **Research needed?** — Should web research be done to gather facts, stats, or references?
6. **Title, author, and tags** — Ask for the article title, author name, and relevant tags.
7. **Save location** — Where should the finished `.md` file be saved?

Do NOT proceed to Phase 2 until all questions are answered.

### Phase 2: Research (if requested)

If the user asked for research:

- Use `WebSearch` to find relevant facts, statistics, expert opinions, and current information.
- Search for multiple perspectives on the topic.
- Gather concrete data points, examples, and quotes that will make the article credible.
- Note your sources — you may reference them naturally in the article.
- Spend time here. Research is 80% of the work. The more you know, the better you write.

If the user said no research is needed, skip to Phase 3.

### Phase 3: Outline

Create a structured outline and present it to the user for approval:

- Start with the core thesis or main takeaway.
- Break the article into logical sections with clear headings.
- Under each heading, note the key points to cover.
- Ensure the flow follows the inverted pyramid — most important information first, supporting details after.
- The outline should read like a table of contents with bullet points under each section.

Wait for user approval before proceeding. Adjust the outline if the user requests changes.

### Phase 4: Write

Write the full article following the methodology below. This is the core of the skill.

#### Step 1 — Know Your Audience

Shape every sentence for the specific reader. A developer audience gets different language than a general audience. Match their vocabulary, their concerns, their level of expertise. If they're beginners, explain. If they're experts, respect what they already know.

#### Step 2 — Stay Focused

One article, one clear purpose. Don't try to cover everything. Narrow the scope to a single, well-defined topic. Every paragraph should serve that focus. If a sentence doesn't support the main point, cut it.

#### Step 3 — Lead With a Strong Introduction

The first few sentences decide whether someone keeps reading. Hook the reader with:
- A surprising fact or statistic
- A relatable problem or question
- A bold statement or observation
- A short, vivid story

Answer the who, what, when, where, why, and how early. Don't bury the lead.

#### Step 4 — Use Simple, Clear Language

- Aim for 20 words or fewer per sentence on average.
- Choose short, everyday words over complex ones.
- If a simpler word works, use it. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "Start" not "commence."
- Cut any word that doesn't earn its place.

#### Step 5 — Write Conversationally

Write like you're talking to the reader. Not at them — to them.
- Use "you" and "your" naturally.
- Use contractions: "don't", "won't", "here's", "it's".
- Say "Here's what you need to know" instead of "One must consider the following."
- Ask rhetorical questions to keep the reader engaged.
- Let your personality come through.

#### Step 6 — Use Active Voice

Active voice is stronger, clearer, and more direct.
- "The team built the feature" not "The feature was built by the team."
- "We found three problems" not "Three problems were found."
- Passive voice is okay occasionally for emphasis or variety, but active should be the default.

#### Step 7 — Structure for Readability

- Short paragraphs — 2 to 4 sentences max.
- Use subheadings to break up long sections.
- Use bullet points and numbered lists for scannable information.
- Vary sentence length — mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones.
- If a sentence runs past 25 words, consider splitting it.
- White space is your friend. Let the content breathe.

#### Step 8 — Be Specific, Not Generic

Every claim should have teeth. Don't say "this is really important" — explain why it matters. Don't say "many companies" — name one or give a number. Concrete details build trust. Vague generalities sound like filler.

#### Step 9 — Edit Ruthlessly

After writing the first draft:
1. **First pass — Structure**: Does the article flow logically? Are sections in the right order?
2. **Second pass — Clarity**: Is every sentence clear? Can anything be said more simply?
3. **Third pass — Tone**: Does it sound human? Read it aloud mentally. Would you say this to someone?
4. **Final pass — Anti-patterns**: Check against the anti-patterns list in `references/ANTI_PATTERNS.md`. Remove any AI-sounding phrases that slipped in.

### Phase 5: Deliver

1. **Generate frontmatter** automatically:
   - `title`: From user input (Phase 1)
   - `slug`: Auto-generate from title (lowercase, hyphens, no special chars)
   - `author`: From user input (Phase 1)
   - `date`: Today's date in YYYY-MM-DD format
   - `tags`: From user input (Phase 1)
   - `coverImage`: Leave as empty string `""`
   - `excerpt`: Auto-generate a 1-2 sentence summary of the article
   - `readingTime`: Calculate based on ~200 words per minute
   - `status`: Always `"draft"`

2. **Assemble the final Markdown file** using this structure:

```markdown
---
title: "Article Title Here"
slug: "article-title-here"
author: "Author Name"
date: "YYYY-MM-DD"
tags: ["tag1", "tag2"]
coverImage: ""
excerpt: "A concise summary of the article."
readingTime: 8
status: "draft"
---

Article content here with proper Markdown formatting.

## Section Heading

Content with short paragraphs, lists, and subheadings.

> Blockquotes for callouts and important quotes.

- Bullet points for scannable content
- Like this
```

3. **Save** the file to the location specified by the user in Phase 1.
4. **Present a summary**: title, word count, reading time, and the file path.

## Tone & Voice Rules

These rules apply to EVERY article:

- **Conversational, not casual.** Friendly and approachable but still polished. Think "smart friend explaining something" — not textbook, not slang.
- **Confident, not arrogant.** State things clearly. Don't hedge with "I think" or "perhaps" unless genuine uncertainty exists.
- **Specific, not vague.** Every paragraph should teach something concrete or advance an argument.
- **Varied rhythm.** Mix sentence lengths. Short sentences punch. Longer ones carry nuance and flow. Monotonous rhythm sounds robotic.
- **Reader-focused.** The reader's needs come first. What do they want to know? What problem are they solving? Write for them, not for yourself.

## Anti-Pattern Enforcement

Before delivering any article, read `references/ANTI_PATTERNS.md` and verify the article contains NONE of the listed patterns. This is mandatory — not optional.

Common violations to catch:
- Opening with "In today's rapidly evolving..." or any variation
- Using "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "Additionally" as paragraph starters
- Ending with "In conclusion" followed by a summary of everything already said
- Hedging phrases like "It's important to note that..."
- Words like "leverage", "utilize", "facilitate", "robust", "seamless"

If any are found during the edit pass, rewrite those sections.

## Hard Rules

1. **NEVER** use AI filler phrases. Read the anti-patterns reference before every delivery.
2. **NEVER** start the article with "In today's..." or "In the world of..." or any variant.
3. **NEVER** end with "In conclusion" followed by restating all points.
4. **NEVER** use the word "delve" or "dive deep" or "unpack."
5. **ALWAYS** use active voice as the default. Passive is the exception.
6. **ALWAYS** use contractions naturally — "don't", "it's", "won't", "here's".
7. **ALWAYS** ask clarifying questions before writing (Phase 1 is mandatory).
8. **ALWAYS** present an outline for approval before drafting (Phase 3 is mandatory).
9. **ALWAYS** ask the user for the save location before writing the file.
10. **ALWAYS** calculate readingTime from actual word count (words / 200, rounded up).
11. **NEVER** pad articles with filler to hit a word count. Shorter and tighter is better than longer and fluffier.
12. **NEVER** use exclamation marks excessively. One per article maximum, if at all.
