# AI Writing Anti-Patterns

This is the mandatory checklist. Before delivering any article, scan the full text against every pattern below. If any are found, rewrite those sections.

---

## 1. Filler Phrases — Remove or Replace

These phrases add no meaning. They're padding. Cut them entirely or replace with something specific.

| AI Pattern | What to Do |
|---|---|
| "In today's rapidly evolving world/landscape" | Cut. Start with the actual point. |
| "It's worth noting that..." | Cut. Just state the thing. |
| "It goes without saying..." | If it goes without saying, don't say it. |
| "At the end of the day..." | Cut. Say what you mean directly. |
| "In order to" | Replace with "to". |
| "Due to the fact that" | Replace with "because". |
| "It is important to note that" | Cut. State the fact directly. |
| "As a matter of fact" | Cut. Just state the fact. |
| "For all intents and purposes" | Cut entirely. |
| "Needless to say" | Then don't say it. Cut. |
| "When it comes to [topic]" | Cut. Start with the topic directly. |
| "In the realm of" | Cut. Name the topic directly. |
| "At this point in time" | Replace with "now" or "currently". |
| "With that being said" | Cut or replace with "That said,". |
| "Having said that" | Replace with "That said," or cut. |
| "It should be noted that" | Cut. Just note it. |
| "The fact of the matter is" | Cut. State the fact. |

## 2. Overused Transitions — Vary Your Connections

These transitions scream "AI-generated" when they appear at the start of every paragraph.

**Never use these as paragraph openers more than once per article:**
- Furthermore
- Moreover
- Additionally
- In addition
- Consequently
- Subsequently
- Accordingly
- Nevertheless
- Nonetheless

**Better alternatives:**
- Start with the subject of the sentence directly.
- Use a short bridge sentence: "That's only part of the story." / "But there's a catch."
- Use a question: "So what does this mean in practice?"
- Use contrast: "On the other hand..." / "But here's the thing..."
- Just start the next paragraph. You don't always need a transition.

## 3. Generic Statements — Replace With Specifics

AI writing defaults to vague, sweeping claims. Every statement should be specific.

| Generic (Bad) | Specific (Good) |
|---|---|
| "This is very important" | Explain WHY it matters with a concrete example |
| "Many companies are doing X" | "Stripe, Shopify, and Linear all adopted X in 2024" |
| "This can have a significant impact" | "This reduced page load time by 40% in our tests" |
| "There are many benefits" | List 2-3 specific benefits |
| "This is a game-changer" | Explain what specifically changed and for whom |
| "This has become increasingly popular" | Cite a number or trend |
| "Experts agree that..." | Name the expert or cite the source |
| "Studies show that..." | Name the study or link it |
| "It's no secret that..." | Cut. Just state the thing. |

## 5. Structural Anti-Patterns

### The Predictable Five-Paragraph Essay
AI loves: intro → point 1 → point 2 → point 3 → conclusion that restates intro.
**Fix:** Vary structure. Use narrative flow. Not every article needs the same skeleton.

### The Summary Conclusion
Starting the last section with "In conclusion" or "To sum up" and then restating every point made.
**Fix:** End with a forward-looking thought, a call to action, a question, or a memorable line. Don't recap — the reader just read it.

### Identical Paragraph Lengths
Every paragraph being roughly the same length (3-4 sentences) looks machine-generated.
**Fix:** Vary paragraph length. Some paragraphs are one sentence. Some are five. Match length to the content.

### The Exhaustive List
Listing every possible thing instead of choosing the most relevant 3-5 items.
**Fix:** Curate. Pick the best examples, not all examples.

### The Hedge Sandwich
Surrounding every statement with qualifiers: "It could be argued that perhaps one might consider..."
**Fix:** State your point. If you're not sure, say "I'm not sure about X, but here's what I know."

## 6. Sentence-Level Anti-Patterns

- **Starting sentences with "This"** without a clear referent. "This is important." — What is "this"? Name it.
- **Using "very" as an intensifier.** "Very important" → "critical." "Very large" → "massive." Find a stronger word.
- **Passive voice as default.** "The article was written" → "I wrote the article." Active first.
- **Exclamation marks.** One max per article. Zero is fine. Exclamation marks in articles feel forced.
- **Em dashes overuse.** One or two per article is fine. Five or more looks like a tic.
- **Rhetorical questions in clusters.** One rhetorical question engages. Three in a row annoys. Space them out.

## 7. Opening Line Anti-Patterns

Never open an article with:
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Picture this:"
- "Let's face it:"
- "It's no secret that..."
- "We all know that..."
- "The world of [topic] has changed dramatically..."
- "Imagine a world where..."
- "[Topic] is more important than ever."
- "As we navigate the complexities of..."

**Better openings:**
- A specific fact or statistic
- A short anecdote (2-3 sentences)
- A bold, direct statement
- A question about a real problem the reader faces
- Jump straight into the topic with the first useful point

## 8. Closing Anti-Patterns

Never close an article with:
- "In conclusion..."
- "To wrap things up..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "The future is bright for [topic]."
- "Only time will tell."
- "The possibilities are endless."
- A bullet-point summary of everything you just said.

**Better closings:**
- A forward-looking statement or prediction
- A specific call to action
- A callback to the opening anecdote or hook
- A single memorable line
- A direct recommendation
